## Daniel Turcu - LinkedIn Writing Style Rules

These rules are mandatory for every piece of LinkedIn content: posts, comments, replies, and engagement drafts. No exceptions.

## NEVER Use (Hard Bans)

### Banned Openers
- "Hot take:" - overused, signals performative contrarianism
- "Unpopular opinion:" - same problem
- "I'm going to say something controversial..." - just say it
- "In today's fast-paced..." - dead on arrival
- "As a thought leader..." - if you have to say it, you aren't one

### Banned Punctuation
- Em-dashes (---) - never use them. Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence. This is a hard rule. Not "minimize usage." Never.
- Semicolons in LinkedIn posts - too formal. Split into two sentences.
- Excessive exclamation marks - one per post maximum, and only if genuinely warranted

### Banned Words and Phrases
- "synergy" / "synergize" / "synergistic"
- "leverage" (as a verb meaning "use")
- "paradigm shift"
- "thought leader" / "thought leadership"
- "game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "best-in-class"
- "disruptive" / "disruption" (unless literally describing a hardware interrupt)
- "holistic"
- "ecosystem" (unless referring to an actual software ecosystem like AUTOSAR)
- "circle back"
- "move the needle"
- "at the end of the day"
- "it goes without saying"
- "passionate about" (show it, don't say it)
- "excited to announce" (just announce it)
- "humbled" (you're not humbled, you're proud, say that)
- "learnings" (the word is "lessons")
- "impactful" (describe the actual impact instead)
- "utilize" (the word is "use")

### Banned Content Patterns
- AI-enthusiasm content: "AI will revolutionize...", "the future of...", "AI is transforming..."
- Gratuitous self-congratulation: "I'm proud to share that I..." (reframe around the team or the work)
- LinkedIn engagement bait: "Like if you agree", "Share this with someone who...", "Tag a friend who..."
- Humble-brag framing: "I never expected this post to go viral..."
- Listicles disguised as insight: "5 things every safety engineer should know" (make a specific argument instead)
- Hashtag spam: never more than 3 hashtags, only at the very end of the post, only technically relevant ones

### Banned Hashtag List
- #Leadership #Innovation #Success #Motivation #Growth #MindSet #Entrepreneur
- Any hashtag with more than 1M followers that isn't technically specific

## DO Use (Active Style Rules)

### Voice
- First person singular. "I" not "we" for opinions. "We" only when referring to a specific TIC team action.
- Direct and opinionated. Take a position. "Most companies treat ASPICE as a checkbox exercise. It shows." is better than "ASPICE compliance can sometimes be approached superficially."
- Conversational authority. Write like you're explaining something to a sharp peer at a conference bar after two beers. Not presenting to a board. Not writing a LinkedIn article for clout.

### Sentence Structure
- Short sentences. Fragment sentences work for emphasis. Like this one.
- Vary rhythm. A longer sentence that builds context, followed by a short punch. Then another short one.
- No paragraph longer than 3 sentences in a LinkedIn post. White space matters on mobile.
- First line of any post must stand alone as a feed-scroll stopper.

### Technical Specificity
- Reference specific clause numbers: "Part 6, Clause 9 of ISO 26262" not "the safety standard"
- Name real tools: Vector DaVinci, EB tresos, Lauterbach TRACE32, Timing Architects arti-T, IBM DOORS, Polarion, codebeamer
- Reference real process levels: "ASPICE SWE.3 Level 2" not "a good software design process"
- Use real metrics: "We closed 47 MPU regions in 3 weeks" not "we improved our timing analysis significantly"
- Cite actual standards: UN R155, UN R156, ISO 21434, ISO 26262:2018, ISO/PAS 8926

### Anonymized References
- "At a Tier-1 last month..." is fine
- "On an ASIL-D program we delivered last year..." is fine
- "A client's safety team told me..." is fine
- NEVER name the client, the OEM program, or the specific vehicle platform
- NEVER include budget figures, contract values, or team sizes for specific engagements

### Conversation Starters (Post Endings)
Every post ends with a specific question that invites practitioner-level responses:
- Good: "What's the most expensive evidence gap you've had to close retroactively?"
- Good: "How does your team handle the gap between safety concept and software architecture in practice?"
- Good: "When was the last time a gate review actually stopped a release?"
- Bad: "What do you think?"
- Bad: "Agree?"
- Bad: "Let me know in the comments!"
- Bad: "Thoughts?"

### Tone Calibration

The target tone is: **credible practitioner, not corporate marketer.**

Think: a principal engineer who also runs a business. Someone who's read the standards cover-to-cover AND has shipped the deliverables. Not a marketing department. Not a conference keynote speaker. Not a LinkedIn influencer.

Signs you've nailed it:
- A safety manager reads it and thinks "this person has done the work"
- An assessor reads it and thinks "finally, someone who gets what we actually look for"
- A Tier-1 engineering director reads it and thinks "I should talk to this person about our program"

Signs you've missed:
- It could have been written by anyone with a LinkedIn account and ChatGPT
- It sounds like a consulting firm's "insights" page
- It's optimized for likes instead of credibility
